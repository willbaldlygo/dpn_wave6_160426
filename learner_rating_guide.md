# Learner Rating Guide
## AI Bootcamp Check-In Response Scoring

This guide explains the two scoring frameworks used to assess learner check-in responses to the "How have you been using AI?" quiz family. It is designed to allow manual scoring of individual responses using the same principles applied in the automated analysis.

---

## Overview: Two Frameworks, Two Purposes

| Framework | Applied to | Purpose |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Sophistication Score (1–4)** | Each individual quiz response | Tracks change over time — how deeply is AI embedded in this person's practice *this week*? |
| **Bloom's Taxonomy Level (L1–L6)** | Each learner overall | Measures the highest cognitive level evidenced across *all* their responses |

These answer different questions. Sophistication tells you about trajectory and progression. Bloom tells you about ceiling — the most sophisticated thinking this learner has demonstrated at any point.

---

## Part 1: Sophistication Score (1–4)

Apply this score to **each individual response** from a single check-in.

### Score 1 — Basic / Passive

The learner describes minimal, low-stakes, or personal AI use. There is no professional context, or the response explicitly reports not engaging with AI this week.

**Indicators:**
- Personal or leisure use only (holiday planning, recipes, travel itineraries, shopping)
- Self-reported non-use: "I haven't used it much this week", "same as last week", "very little"
- Single trivial task with no work relevance
- Very short response (under 30 words) with no substantive content

**Examples from the data:**
> "Used it over Christmas for personal use as been off work on holiday. Used it to provide history information on places that we visited."

> "Only used for checking paragraphs to increase accuracy, factualness, creativity."

> "I haven't been as active on it this week as work has been very busy. Been looking at its suggestions for my project idea... Did ask it to help me plan a little city break to Faro though."

---

### Score 2 — Applied

The learner is using AI for real, named professional tasks — but in a discrete, episodic way. Each use is a standalone task rather than part of a repeatable system or ongoing workflow.

**Indicators:**
- Specific work context named (client, meeting, report, website, correspondence, research)
- A real professional output described (letter, blog post, policy draft, meeting summary)
- Single-session or one-off use — no sense that this is becoming a habit or workflow
- Response is substantive (40+ words) and professionally grounded

**Examples from the data:**
> "I have asked AI with help in preparing correspondence to give the correct tone etc.; help with improving an existing website page to make it more reader friendly and up to date."

> "I've used it to help write an opinion piece for a client — I wrote the piece first and then put the text into Gemini to reduce the number of words."

> "Using Tangent Templates — creating products to sell on Amazon Kindle. Including artwork with logo and mascots for my intended items to be published."

---

### Score 3 — Integrated

AI is embedded in ongoing professional work. The learner describes repeatable workflows, custom tools in regular use, multi-task sessions, or continuing projects. There is a sense that AI is now part of how they work rather than something they occasionally try.

**Indicators:**
- A custom Gem or CustomGPT described as being created, set up, or regularly used
- NotebookLM with curated sources for a specific research or learning project
- Multiple distinct work tasks described in a single response (suggesting AI is embedded across their work)
- An ongoing project being developed or continued week-on-week
- Uploaded documents being processed for professional outputs (meeting minutes, PDFs, spreadsheets)
- Professional workflows being designed, documented or improved with AI assistance
- Advanced research tools in use (STORM, Deep Research, structured NotebookLM projects)

**Examples from the data:**
> "I have set up a couple of research based Gems in Google Gemini so that I can regularly check in on new stats and research."

> "Used NotebookLM with PDFs of Wills, Estate Planning and Inheritance tax textbooks as well as searching for appropriate additional web resources and HMRC tools. The program has been teaching me the rules for a new area of law."

> "Working with Copilot to review our current grant giving processes with a view to improving the structure of the grant application form, the grant process including due diligence, and drafting a grant giving policy."

---

### Score 4 — Systemic

AI is operating at system level. The learner is building tools or pipelines that go beyond their own use — for clients, colleagues, or commercial purposes. Alternatively, they are deploying multi-tool strategies, automating processes end-to-end, or teaching AI skills to others.

**Indicators:**
- Building an app, chatbot, or multi-feature tool (for themselves *or* others to use)
- Using Zapier, Make, Power Automate, or similar for process automation
- Deliberately using two or more AI tools in sequence (output of one feeds into another)
- Describing commercial AI product development (Amazon Kindle, client-facing systems, marketable tools)
- Building tools for colleagues or clients to use
- Teaching AI skills to others in their organisation
- Describing a system or pipeline with multiple components, not just a single prompt

**Examples from the data:**
> "Created a Non-Conformance Reporting app where team members can report an issue, either by voice or text, ready for management to review, complete the root cause analysis and plan actions. The app includes a table view of all issues, with status and a report."

> "I created a chat bot for a children's book author. The main character... I designed a chat bot that first asks for your name. Then it asks you to input a date... it will then come up with 5 inspirational ideas..."

> "Reviewing our grant application process and working with different automation platforms e.g. Zapier to make the process slicker and requiring less manual intervention."

> "I first tried to use ChatGPT, loading company financial information... This produced a result but was not quite what I wanted. I then put the same inputs together with the Chat outputs into Claude and asked it to address the issues."

---

### Scoring Edge Cases

**"Same as last week" responses with some new content**
If a response begins with a low-engagement phrase but goes on to describe substantive new activity, score based on the new content, not the opener. The override to Score 1 only applies when the response *is* genuinely thin (under 80 words with no new professional content).

**Personal use alongside professional use**
Score based on the highest level of use described in the response. If a learner describes automating a client process *and* planning a holiday, the professional content determines the score.

**Ambiguous between 2 and 3**
Ask: is there any evidence of repeatability, customisation, or ongoing development? A one-off professional task is 2. The same task described as part of a regular workflow or custom tool is 3.

## Part 2: Bloom's Taxonomy Overall Score (L1–L6)

Apply this score **once per learner**, based on reading all their responses together. The score reflects the **highest level of cognitive engagement evidenced at any point** — consistent with the framework's own guidance that assessment should be based on the highest level evidenced, not an average.

The Bloom levels below are drawn directly from your *Artefacts and Activities Mapped to Bloom's Taxonomy* document, interpreted for application to self-reported free-text responses rather than physical artefacts.

---

### L1 — Remember

The learner names tools and describes basic activities but shows no evidence of understanding why AI works as it does or applying it to professional tasks.

**Look for:** Lists of tools used, brief descriptions of trying something, no professional context, no explanation or reflection.

**Example response profile:** "I've been using ChatGPT and Gemini. I asked it to write a poem for my daughter's birthday."

---

### L2 — Understand

The learner demonstrates conceptual understanding — they can explain why AI behaves as it does, reflect on differences between tools, or articulate what AI is and isn't good for.

**Look for:** Explanations using "because" or "why", reflections on AI behaviour (hallucination, output differences between tools), accurate use of AI terminology in context, a sense that they understand the *mechanism* behind what they're using.

**Example response profile:** "I've realised that AI gives different answers depending on how you phrase the question, so I've been more careful about how I structure my prompts."

**Special case — Strategic Learner:** A senior manager who articulates a deliberate strategy for deploying AI across their organisation (delegating implementation to team members while attending for strategic oversight) may score L4 even if their personal AI use is minimal. This reflects analytical reasoning about AI deployment at an organisational level. Flag this as "strategic Bloom" in your reasoning.

---

### L3 — Apply

The learner consistently uses AI to complete real professional tasks. AI is being put to practical use, and the responses describe functional outputs. There is no evidence yet of system-building, comparative analysis, or iterative refinement.

**Look for:** Regular professional use across multiple responses, a range of work tasks described, clear practical outputs (reports, letters, social posts, meeting summaries). No evidence of building custom tools or critically comparing AI outputs.

**Example response profile:** Multiple responses describing using AI for client correspondence, website copy, research tasks — functional and professional throughout, but no system design.

---

### L4 — Analyse

The learner breaks problems into components and examines relationships — between tools, between inputs and outputs, between AI behaviour and context. This is evidenced by comparison, diagnosis, or critical reflection on *why* AI performed as it did.

**Look for:**
- Running the same task through two different AI tools and comparing the outputs
- Diagnosing *why* an AI output was wrong or suboptimal
- Critical commentary on AI reliability, accuracy or limitations
- Understanding the relationship between prompt quality and output quality
- Identifying what AI is *not* good at, with specific reasoning

**Example response profile:** "I found that ChatGPT gave me outdated information on the policy, but Gemini picked up the inaccuracies. I'll cross-check everything from now on."

---

### L5 — Create

The learner designs and builds original AI-powered tools or systems. This goes beyond using taught techniques — they are constructing something that didn't exist before, using AI as a component.

**Look for:**
- Building a custom Gem, CustomGPT, or chatbot (even if imperfect)
- Constructing an automation workflow or pipeline
- Setting up a curated NotebookLM knowledge base configured for a specific purpose
- Building an app using AI Studio, Lovable, or similar tools
- Designing a reusable prompt template, schema or data structure
- Creating a tool that others will use

**Example response profile:** "I've built a Gem in Gemini that acts as a business coach to support me in setting up a new CIC. I've also set up a home tech support Gem I used this week to set up my new laptop."

---

### L6 — Evaluate

The learner tests their systems, identifies failure points, and refines based on evidence. This is the defining behaviour: a cycle of build → test → identify problem → adapt. They are not just using AI or building with it — they are critically assessing fitness for purpose and improving accordingly.

**Look for:**
- Restarting or significantly revising a project after identifying a flaw ("I realised I hadn't specified it sufficiently so ended up re-starting")
- Refining prompts or systems based on observed output quality
- Explicitly testing AI outputs before using them consequentially ("still needs double checking", "validate before submission")
- Describing a failure, naming the cause, and describing the adaptation
- Comparing tools on the same task to assess relative fitness
- Explicitly critiquing AI accuracy, reliability or integrity with specific evidence

**Example response profile:** "My feeling is that it likes putting things in just to tick a box — it actually doesn't have integrity. It picked some Alice in Wonderland cartoon instead of what I'd asked for. So this week I am going to break it down to much smaller chunks and see if it can handle that."

## Scoring Workflow

When scoring a learner manually, work through these steps:

1. **Read all responses first** before assigning any scores. Get a sense of the arc.

2. **Score each response for Sophistication (1–4)** using the rubric above. Note one or two words of reasoning for each (e.g. "custom Gem in use" or "personal travel use only").

3. **Identify the strongest response** — the one that shows the highest level of cognitive engagement. This is likely (but not always) the highest sophistication score.

4. **Check for Bloom L6 signals** in any response: did the learner describe testing, failure diagnosis, iteration, or pre-use verification? Even a single well-evidenced instance is sufficient for L6.

5. **Check for Bloom L5 signals**: did the learner build or design an original tool, workflow or system at any point?

6. **Assign the overall Bloom level** based on the highest level clearly evidenced. If you find L6 signals, score L6 regardless of what the rest of the responses show. Record your reasoning, noting which specific response and behaviour triggered the level.

7. **Flag edge cases**: thin responses on a high-capability learner (mark as low-engagement week, not ceiling); strategic learners (flag explicitly); learners with only one response (note "insufficient longitudinal data").

## Quick Reference Card

### Sophistication (per response)
| Score | One-line test |
|-------|--------------|
| 1 | Would I describe this as "trying AI out" or "not really using it"? |
| 2 | Is there a real work task with a named professional output? |
| 3 | Is AI embedded in something ongoing, repeated, or customised? |
| 4 | Is the learner building for others, automating, or chaining tools? |

### Bloom (per learner, highest evidenced)
| Level | One-line test |
|-------|--------------|
| L1 | Do they only list tools and describe trying things? |
| L2 | Do they explain *why* AI works as it does? |
| L3 | Do they use AI consistently and professionally across multiple tasks? |
| L4 | Do they compare, diagnose, or critically analyse AI behaviour? |
| L5 | Have they built or designed an original AI-powered tool or system? |
| L6 | Have they tested, found a problem, and adapted based on evidence? |

---

*Note: Bloom scoring is based on the Artefacts and Activities Mapped to Bloom's Taxonomy document provided. The activity descriptors at each level (rather than the artefact descriptors) are the primary reference for scoring self-reported text responses, since artefacts themselves are not available for direct assessment.*
